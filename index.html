Debt Literacy

Debt can certainly build up very quickly and overwhelm you if you aren’t careful. In this section, we will explore how to manage existing debt and how to choose and pay future debt to potentially save thousands.

How Debt Works: Simple and Compound Interest

To understand debt, first you have to understand how debt works, and how it can build up so quickly. Simply put, debt is money that you owe to another person or entity. For example, if you borrow $20 from your friend, you are now in debt to him/her for $20. Common types of debt include student loans, mortgages/car loans, and credit card debt.

Many types of debt charge interest, which is additional money that you owe for taking out the loan on top of your original amount. The original amount that you take out is called the principal, and the interest rate is called the annual percentage rate, or just APR.

For example, say you have a loan with principal $1,000 with an annual percentage rate of 10%.

After one year, you will owe the original $1,000, and with an interest of $1000 * 10% = $100 extra, totalling to $1,100. That’s $100 extra that you owe just from interest alone. And the more debt you owe, and the higher the APR, the more you will owe. Say you hold the debt for four more years, and take on $100 more in debt per year, making a debt of $1,500.

However, it can get even worse. The previous scenario was an example of simple interest, where interest is only based on the original principal. Therefore, every year that you don’t pay back the debt, you will owe another $100 in interest. That’s a lot, but it could get worse with compound interest.

Compound interest is interest charged on the principal, but also on any interest that you owe. An example will help clarify, so let us relate back to the original example, with the principal debt of $1,000 and the APR of 10%, only this time it is compounded.

Year 1: After the first year, you will still owe the original $1,000 plus the interest of $1,000 * 10% = $100, for a total of $1,100.

Year 2: However, this is where compound interest differs. For the second year, instead of owing another $100 in interest, you owe $1,100 * 10% = $110, for a total debt of $1,210.

Did you see what happened? The 10% interest was charged not only on the principal of $1,000, but also on the $100 interest you accumulated last year!

Year 3: Following the pattern, the next year interest will be $1,210 * 10% = $121, making the total that you owe $1,331.

Year 4: For the fourth year, the interest will be $1,331 * 10% = $133.10, making the total $1,464.10.

Year 5: Finally for the fifth year, the interest will be $1,464.10 * 10% = $146.41, making the total $1,610.51.

Now, compare this total with the total for simple interest. Even with the same principal and APR, you owe an extra $110.51. Additionally, both were considerably higher than the original amount of $1,000.

What if you took both loans and held it for 25 years, instead of the original 5 in the example? For the simple interest loan, you would owe $3,500 total. However, for the compound interest loan, you would owe a staggering $10,834.71! That’s 10 times the original loan!

There are, of course, ways to calculate how much money you would owe without mapping out every single year. Here are the mathematical formulas:

Simple Interest

A = P(1 + rt)
  • A = Total Amount owed (Principal + Interest)
  • P = Principal (original amount borrowed)
  • r = Annual interest rate (as a decimal, e.g., 10% = 0.10)
  • t = Time the money is borrowed for (in years)

Compound Interest

A = P(1 + r/n)nt
  • A = Total Amount owed (Principal + Interest)
  • P = Principal (original amount borrowed)
  • r = Annual interest rate (as a decimal)
  • n = Number of times interest is compounded per year
  • t = Time the money is borrowed for (in years)

Key Takeaways

  • When taking out a loan, the original amount is called the principal, but you will also owe additional interest, determined by the principal and the annual percentage rate (APR).
  • Simple interest charges interest on the principal alone, but compound interest charges on the principal plus any interest accumulated.
  • Compound interest can grow extremely quickly!
  • When taking out a loan, it is critical to check the principal, APR, and whether the loan is simple or compounded.
